\title{Prism: A Physics-Constrained Neural Emulator for Stellar Spectra}

\author[0009-0006-8079-688X]{Aneesh Shastri}
\affiliation{Department of Computational Engineering, Cepheid,
Indian Institute of Technology Hyderabad,
Sangareddy, Telangana 502284, India}
\email{co25btech11004@iith.ac.in}

\begin{abstract}
We present Prism, a physics-constrained generative neural network emulator for stellar spectra
trained directly on real, high SNR observations from the APOGEE DR17 survey.
Unlike prior approaches that rely on synthetic spectral grids, we train our model on
149,966 stars with signal-to-noise ratio $>100$ using stellar parameters and chemical
abundances from the ASPCAP pipeline as labels, spanning a wavelength range of 1514--1694\,nm.

The model predicts optical depth through four physically motivated branches---a continuum
branch, a CNO molecular branch, a thermodynamic state branch, and a chemical expert branch
with element-specific weighted spectral masks sourced from ASPCAP's \texttt{.filt}
files---and converts to flux via the Beer-Lambert law. Physics-based disentanglement is
enforced architecturally by restricting each element's spectral sensitivity to its
permitted spectral windows. We evaluate model performance using a
reduced $\chi^2$ statistic and compare against four baselines:
The Payne, an Expanded Payne, a baseline Ablation Prism, and a regularized Payne (RegPayne).

Prism achieves a median reduced $\chi^2_\mathrm{red} = 1.766$ on a held-out test set of
$\sim$9,966 stars, outperforming all baselines. An information-leakage test confirms that
Prism's gradient response at forbidden pixels is identically zero (as required by its
architecture). Notably, Ablation Prism---which shares Prism's branched architecture but
omits the spectral masks---achieves substantially lower leakage than flat-MLP baselines,
demonstrating that the per-element branch structure itself promotes disentanglement
independently of the hard masking constraint.
The model generalizes and extrapolates well across most parameter ranges, though performance
systematically degrades in cool regions (due to complicated molecular physics) and for hot
stars which lie out of the training distribution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The chemical fingerprint encoded in a stellar photosphere reflects the conditions of the
interstellar medium at the time and place of that star's formation, as well as the integrated
nucleosynthetic enrichment from preceding generations of supernovae and asymptotic giant
branch stars. Since photospheric abundances change negligibly over a stellar lifetime,
high-fidelity abundance measurements from large spectroscopic surveys constitute a powerful
fossil record of Galactic chemical evolution \citep{Freeman2002,BlandHawthorn2016}.

This goal has only recently become tractable as survey facilities deliver high-resolution
($R \gtrsim 20{,}000$) spectra for $\sim 10^5$--$10^6$ stars across all Galactic components
\citep{Majewski2017}. APOGEE (Apache Point Observatory Galactic Evolution Experiment)
in particular has assembled one of the largest homogeneous near-infrared spectroscopic
datasets in existence, with Data Release 17 containing labels derived for hundreds of
thousands of giant stars \citep{Abdurrouf2022}.

Automated pipelines such as ASPCAP (APOGEE Stellar Parameter and Chemical Abundance
Pipeline) have made survey-scale stellar characterization possible, yet physics-based model
spectra are known to be incomplete: one-dimensional model atmospheres, the local
thermodynamic equilibrium approximation, and imperfect atomic and molecular line lists
all introduce systematic residuals \citep{GarciaperezASPCAP2016}. These limitations become
especially acute at low signal-to-noise ratios, where classical $\chi^2$ fitting degrades
rapidly.

Data-driven models offer a complementary approach. The Cannon \citep{Ness2015Cannon}
demonstrated that a quadratic generative model trained on high-fidelity reference spectra
can transfer stellar labels to lower signal-to-noise data with competitive precision. The
Cannon 2 \citep{Casey2016Cannon2} extended this to 17 labels simultaneously by introducing
$\ell_1$ regularization that enforces sparsity in the model's spectral coefficients, yielding
physically interpretable spectral derivatives. The Payne \citep{Ting2019Payne} replaced the
polynomial basis with a multi-layer perceptron trained on grids of synthetic spectra,
achieving high accuracy on APOGEE observations. More recently, TransformerPayne
\citep{TransformerPayne2025} employed large transformer architectures over the same
synthetic-grid paradigm, reporting mean absolute errors of $\sim 10^{-3}$ on held-out
synthetic data.

A common thread in nearly all prior emulators is reliance on synthetic spectral grids
for training. While such grids are noise-free by construction, they encode all the
approximations of the underlying physics model and, critically, are evaluated on real data
that differ systematically from their training distribution. Furthermore, most neural emulators
operate as black boxes: the learned mapping from stellar parameters to predicted flux offers
little interpretable insight into which spectral features are being used, leaving open the
question of parameter cross-talk (entanglement).

In this work, we present a generative neural emulator trained entirely on real APOGEE DR17
spectra. Prism is distinguished by three design principles. First, we predict optical
depth rather than flux directly, using the Beer-Lambert law to recover flux from summed
contributions of physically separated branches. Second, element-specific spectral masks
enforce strict disentanglement: each chemical abundance label is permitted to influence only
those pixels lying within 0.2\,nm of its known atomic lines, as compiled in
\citet{Shetrone2015} and \citet{Smith2021DR17}. Third, because our training set is composed
of real observations with realistic noise, continuum normalization artifacts, and sensor gaps,
the emulator inherits the noise properties of APOGEE data without requiring a synthetic
bridge.

Throughout this work we use the term \emph{label} for any element of the input
parameter vector, including stellar parameters ($T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, etc.) and
chemical abundances ([$X$/Fe] or [$X$/H]).

\subsection{APOGEE DR17}
\label{subsec:data_apogee}

We use spectra and labels from APOGEE Data Release 17 \citep{Abdurrouf2022}. The APOGEE
spectrograph covers the $H$-band (1514--1694\,nm excluding sensor gaps) at resolution $R \approx 22{,}500$.
The ASPCAP pipeline performs continuum normalization and spectral stacking before
data release \citep{GarciaperezASPCAP2016}. We mask sensor gaps (pixels with zero flux) throughout all processing steps.

Our working sample comprises 149,966 stars selected to have signal-to-noise ratio
SNR $> 100$ in the stacked, pseudo-continuum-normalized spectra. We adopt stellar parameters and
23 chemical abundance labels from ASPCAP (see Table~\ref{tab:labels}). Six core
stellar parameters include effective temperature $T_\mathrm{eff}$, surface gravity $\log g$,
overall metallicity $[M/\mathrm{H}]$, microturbulent velocity $v_\mathrm{micro}$,
macroturbulent velocity $v_\mathrm{macro}$, and projected rotational velocity $v \sin i$.
The 17 elemental abundances span the CNO group, the $\alpha$-capture, odd-Z, and Fe-peak
families.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Label Imputation}
\label{subsec:preproc}

Missing label values occur in the dataset because ASPCAP flags individual
abundance fits when signal is insufficient or assigns invalid placeholder values. 
We apply robust filtering logic (flagging values $< -100$ or possessing non-zero ASPCAP error flags)
to accurately identify all missing dimensions. 
Rather than discard affected stars, we recover
missing values via iterative imputation using a Bayesian Ridge regressor that learns
inter-label correlations (e.g., the tight correlation between $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ and
$[M/\mathrm{H}]$) and predicts missing entries from available ones. To strictly prevent information leakage, the imputer is fitted entirely on the training set and subsequently applied to validation and test data without refitting. After imputation,
we convert all [$X$/Fe] ratios to [$X$/H] by adding the imputed $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$
value. Details on missing-value fractions and the imputation procedure are available in 
our project repository. A summary of the parameter missing value percentages is presented in Table~\ref{tab:parameter_missing_values}.

We supplement the raw labels with four engineered features to improve the model's ability to
decode stellar physics:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $5040/T_\mathrm{eff}$ -- encodes the Boltzmann exponential dependence of line opacity
      on excitation temperature; 
\item $[\mathrm{C/H}] - [\mathrm{O/H}]$ -- the C/O ratio, which governs the balance
      between CO molecule formation and free atomic C and O;
\item $v_\mathrm{broad} = \sqrt{v_\mathrm{macro}^2 + (v\sin i)^2}$ -- a single
      combined broadening term;
\item $\log P_e = 0.5[\mathrm{M/H}]+0.5\log{g}$ -- a first-order approximation of electron pressure derived from
      metallicity and surface gravity, which modulates continuous opacity.
\end{enumerate}

We apply $z$-score normalization to all labels (raw and engineered) using training-set statistics.
Spectral fluxes are used as delivered by ASPCAP (already pseudo-continuum normalized).

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:model}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Optical-depth Formulation}
\label{subsec:tau}

Rather than regressing directly to continuum-normalized flux, our model predicts the
effective optical depth $\tau(\lambda)$ and converts to flux via the Beer-Lambert law:
\begin{equation}
F(\lambda) = k \, e^{-\tau(\lambda)},
\label{eq:beer}
\end{equation}

where $k = k(\lambda)$ is a wavelength-dependent scaling factor produced by the continuum branch. We chose a scaling factor instead of another optical depth term to address ASPCAP's continuum normalization artifacts (which occasionally produce normalized fluxes slightly above unity near absorption features).

The total optical depth is additive in contributions from
physically distinct components:
\begin{equation}
\tau(\lambda) =  \tau_\mathrm{CNO}(\lambda)
               + \sum_X \tau_X(\lambda),
\label{eq:tau_sum}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over all atomic species $X$ in Table~\ref{tab:labels} (excluding the
CNO group), and each term is produced by a dedicated network branch.

\subsection{Network Branches}
\label{subsec:branches}

\subsubsection{Continuum Branch}
The continuum branch receives the global parameters:
$T_{\text{eff}},[\mathrm{M/H}],\log{g},v_{\text{macro}},v_{\text{micro}},v\sin{i}$,
plus all engineered features. Using these inputs, it generates a coarse 268-pixel continuum
that we progressively upsample via alternating upsampling and Conv1D layers to the full
8575-pixel grid. The final convolutional layer uses a kernel of width 51 pixels, ensuring
the branch produces smoothly varying pseudo-continuum and is structurally incapable of
fitting sharp spectral lines. This architectural constraint encodes the physical fact that
true stellar continua vary smoothly on scales much larger than individual line widths
(${\sim}0.01$\,nm at $R \approx 22{,}500$).

\subsubsection{CNO (Molecular) Branch}
The CNO branch receives $[\mathrm{C/H}]$, $[\mathrm{N/H}]$, $[\mathrm{O/H}]$,
$T_\mathrm{eff}$, and $\log g$. Because molecular line lists (CO, CN, OH, etc.) are
incomplete and thousands of transitions contribute at every wavelength, we intentionally leave
this branch unconstrained by pixel masks. It uses stacked dense layers to predict
$\tau_\mathrm{CNO}$ across the full wavelength array.

\subsubsection{Thermodynamic State Branch}
The thermodynamic state branch distills all global and CNO parameters into a compact
latent state vector of dimension 8. We pass this state vector to the chemical expert
branch rather than raw label values, preventing the chemical branch from using
$[M/\mathrm{H}]$ as a proxy for individual elemental abundances. This architectural
choice is our primary mechanism for enforcing physically correct label--flux correlations.
We note that this state vector does not directly correspond to any named physical quantity;
it is a learned low-dimensional representation of the thermodynamic context in which
atomic line formation occurs.

\subsubsection{Chemical Expert Branch}
Each atomic species $X$ has its own dedicated expert block. The block concatenates the
thermodynamic state vector with a single scalar $[X/\mathrm{H}]$ and passes the result
through two dense layers (64 units each), followed by a sparse spectral window projection.
We define the spectral windows using ASPCAP's \texttt{.filt} files
\citep{GarciaperezASPCAP2016,Abdurrouf2022},
which provide continuous-valued significance weights for each pixel--species pair rather
than binary on/off masks. The \texttt{SparseProjector} layer scatters the expert's local
optical depth prediction into the full 8575-pixel array, weighting each pixel by its
species-specific significance. Pixels with zero weight are structurally excluded from the
output:
\begin{equation}
\tau_X(\lambda_i) = w_{X,i} \, f_X\bigl(\mathbf{s}, [X/\mathrm{H}]\bigr),
\label{eq:mask}
\end{equation}
where $w_{X,i}$ is the ASPCAP window weight for species $X$ at pixel $i$,
$\mathbf{s}$ is the thermodynamic state vector, and $f_X$ is the expert sub-network.

This weighting constraint is mathematically hard: gradients cannot propagate through
zero-weight pixels, so the network is provably unable to attribute spectral signal at a
non-permitted wavelength to species $X$.

\subsection{Training}
\label{subsec:training}

We train the model end-to-end using the Adam optimizer with gradient clipping
($\|\nabla\|_{\max} = 1.0$) and an initial learning rate of $10^{-3}$. We partition the APOGEE
dataset into training (120,000 stars), validation (20,000 stars), and test
($\sim$9,966 stars) sets. We mask sensor-gap pixels (identified by zero flux or zero inverse
variance) from the loss throughout training. The model trains with a batch size of 256 for
up to 150 epochs. Three callbacks regulate convergence:
\texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} (patience 2, factor 0.5), \texttt{EarlyStopping} (patience
10, monitoring validation loss), and \texttt{ModelCheckpoint} saving the best-performing
weights. We train all baselines using identical callbacks for fair comparison.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Loss and Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:loss}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Training Loss: Spectral Pixel Loss}
\label{subsec:spl_loss}

We define the training objective as a weighted mean squared error computed per pixel:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{spl} = \frac{1}{N_\mathrm{valid}} \sum_{i}
  w_i \bigl(F_{\mathrm{pred},i} - F_{\mathrm{obs},i}\bigr)^2,
\label{eq:spl_loss}
\end{equation}
where the per-pixel weight is
\begin{equation}
w_i = \frac{10^{-5}}{1/\mathit{ivar}_i + 10^{-5}},
\label{eq:spl_weight}
\end{equation}
and $\mathit{ivar}_i$ is the APOGEE inverse variance at pixel $i$. This weight smoothly
transitions from zero (where $\mathit{ivar} = 0$, i.e., sensor gaps) to unity (where
signal-to-noise is high), and the $10^{-5}$ floor term prevents numerical instabilities.
We additionally zero out pixels with observed flux below a bad-pixel cutoff
($F_\mathrm{obs} < 10^{-3}$) to exclude defective pixels.

\subsection{Monitoring Metric: Reduced $\chi^2$}
\label{subsec:chi2_metric}

During training, a Keras metric computes a batch-level estimate of the reduced
$\chi^2$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\chi}^2_\mathrm{red} = \frac{1}{\nu} \sum_{i}
  \mathit{ivar}_i \, \bigl(F_{\mathrm{pred},i} - F_{\mathrm{obs},i}\bigr)^2,
\label{eq:chi2_batch}
\end{equation}
where $\nu$ is the number of valid pixels per star and we use raw
$\mathit{ivar}$ (not the scaled weight of Equation~\ref{eq:spl_weight}). This metric
provides physically interpretable goodness-of-fit during training without affecting
gradient updates.

\subsection{Test-time Evaluation: Reduced $\chi^2$}
\label{subsec:chi2_full}

For final model evaluation on the held-out test set, we compute a per-star reduced
$\chi^2$ using instrumental variance plus a systematic variance floor:
\begin{equation}
\chi^2_{\mathrm{red},\star} = \frac{1}{\nu_\star} \sum_{i=1}^{N_\mathrm{pix}}
  \frac{\bigl(F_{\mathrm{pred},i} - F_{\mathrm{obs},i}\bigr)^2}
       {\sigma^2_{\mathrm{instr},i}  + \sigma^2_\mathrm{floor}},
\label{eq:chi2_full}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^2_\mathrm{instr} = 1/\mathit{ivar}$ is the instrumental variance and
$\sigma^2_\mathrm{floor} = 10^{-5}$ accounts for systematic uncertainties not captured
by ASPCAP's reported $\mathit{ivar}$, including continuum normalization artifacts and
residual telluric contamination at incorrectly flagged pixels. This value is conservative
relative to the ${\sim}0.5$--1\% fractional flux calibration uncertainty reported for the
APOGEE instrument \citep{GarciaperezASPCAP2016,Abdurrouf2022}, which would correspond to
$\sigma^2_\mathrm{floor} \sim 2.5\times10^{-5}$--$10^{-4}$ in normalized flux units.
The degrees of freedom $\nu_\star$ equal the number of valid pixels minus the number of
input labels. This formulation is identical for all models, ensuring a fair cross-model
comparison independent of architectural differences.

\subsection{Information Leakage Test}
\label{subsec:leakage}

An information-leakage test quantifies how much gradient power an element produces at
wavelengths where it has no known spectral lines. For each of the 14 atomic species, we
compute the sensitivity gradient $\partial F / \partial [X/\mathrm{H}]$ via forward-mode
automatic differentiation over the full test set and partition pixels into \emph{allowed}
(non-zero ASPCAP window weight) and \emph{forbidden} (zero weight) regions. The leakage
ratio is defined as $\bar{|\nabla|}_\mathrm{forbidden} \,/\,\bar{|\nabla|}_\mathrm{allowed}$;
a value well below unity indicates that the model concentrates its sensitivity on the
correct spectral windows, while a value above unity suggests the model is attributing more
spectral signal to forbidden pixels than to the element's own transitions---indicative of
abundance entanglement.

Prism achieves leakage ratios of exactly 0.000 across all 14 elements
(Table~\ref{tab:leakage}), as guaranteed by its hard masking architecture. We note that
this result is a direct consequence of the zero-weight constraint in
Equation~(\ref{eq:mask}) rather than a learned property: gradients cannot propagate through
zero-weight pixels by construction, so the zero-leakage result verifies that the
architectural constraint operates as intended rather than providing independent evidence of
physics learning.

The more informative result is that Ablation Prism---which shares Prism's per-element
branch structure but omits the spectral masks---achieves leakage ratios of ${\sim}$0.18--0.73
across most elements, substantially below the ${\sim}$0.61--1.08 range of flat-MLP baselines
(The Payne and Expanded Payne) and the ${\sim}$0.41--1.06 range of RegPayne. This
demonstrates that the per-element branched architecture itself promotes spectral
disentanglement without explicit masking: the data-driven structure of the model recovers
approximate spectral selectivity from the training signal alone, consistent with the atomic
physics encoded in real APOGEE spectra.

The notable exception is Potassium (K), for which Ablation Prism exhibits a leakage ratio
of 4.61---the model places more gradient power at forbidden pixels than at K's own
transitions. This is consistent with K having only two weak lines in the APOGEE bandpass,
making its signal difficult to disentangle from correlated Fe-peak abundances without
architectural enforcement.

All unconstrained flat-MLP baselines (The Payne, Expanded Payne, RegPayne) exhibit leakage
ratios near or above unity for the strongest absorbers (Fe, Mg), confirming that these
models learn unphysical cross-element correlations that Prism's architecture eliminates.

\section{Comparison with Prior Work}
\label{sec:comparison}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The Cannon and The Cannon 2}
\label{subsec:cannon}

The Cannon \citep{Ness2015Cannon} and The Cannon 2 \citep{Casey2016Cannon2} are
data-driven models that fit a polynomial (quadratic) generative function relating stellar
labels to spectral fluxes. The Cannon 2 uses $\ell_1$ regularization to enforce sparsity
in the model's spectral derivatives, which---like our spectral masks---prevents label
coefficients from acquiring unphysical non-zero values at all wavelengths. Our model shares
this philosophy of enforcing physical sparsity but improves upon it in two respects: first,
the sparsity in Prism is mathematically exact (hard masking) rather than approximate (soft
regularization); second, our non-linear neural architecture can capture higher-order
interactions between labels and spectra that a quadratic model cannot represent.

\subsection{The Payne}
\label{subsec:payne}

The Payne \citep{Ting2019Payne} is a multi-layer perceptron emulator trained on 2000
synthetic spectrograms with 7500 pixels, achieving a root-mean-square error of $10^{-3}$
on synthetic validation data. Because it is trained exclusively on synthetic spectra, it
is an \emph{ab initio} model: it can, in principle, return labels on any physical scale,
but it encodes all the approximations of the theoretical atmosphere grid. By contrast,
our model is trained on real APOGEE spectra and therefore learns the empirical noise floor
and calibration characteristics of the instrument.

We trained a Payne-architecture baseline on the same APOGEE data and data split as Prism.
On the held-out test set, the Payne baseline achieves a median $\chi^2_\mathrm{red}
= 2.905$, compared with Prism's $1.766$---a 39\% improvement. The Payne baseline also
suffers from leakage ratios near unity for all atomic species, confirming that it does not
disentangle abundance labels from each other.

\subsection{TransformerPayne}
\label{subsec:transformerpayne}

TransformerPayne \citep{TransformerPayne2025} employs large transformer attention
mechanisms to capture long-range correlations across the full spectrum, again trained on
synthetic grids, and reports an MAE of $\sim 10^{-3}$ on a held-out synthetic dataset.
While this represents excellent performance on synthetic data, it requires substantially
more computational resources than Prism and does not include real-data validation.
Our approach achieves comparable fitting accuracy on real observations with lower
computational cost and with physically enforced interpretability.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison: Expanded Payne, Ablation Prism, and RegPayne}
\label{subsec:baselines}

In addition to the original Payne architecture, which was intentionally left without hyperparameter fine-tuning to serve as a structurally identical baseline, we trained three further models on the same data split. Both the Expanded Payne and RegPayne were systematically fine-tuned using Optuna to maximize their baseline performance:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Expanded Payne} --- a wider multi-layer perceptron with larger hidden layers,
      increasing parameter capacity without adding physics constraints, fine-tuned via Optuna;
\item \textbf{Ablation Prism} --- identical Prism architecture but without the spectral
      masks, isolating the contribution of the per-element branch structure from the
      hard masking constraint;
\item \textbf{RegPayne} --- a Payne-like model fine-tuned via Optuna and trained with a two-phase Jacobian penalty
      schedule (phase 1: 5 epochs, $\lambda_J = 0$; phase 2: full training,
      $\lambda_J = 400$) using the \texttt{build\_disentangled\_model} framework.
\end{enumerate}

Results are summarized in Tables~\ref{tab:chi2} and \ref{tab:chi2_stellar}. Several
observations are noteworthy. First, Ablation Prism (median $\chi^2_\mathrm{red} = 1.932$)
outperforms both Expanded Payne (2.136) and RegPayne (1.994), despite having no
architectural disentanglement constraint beyond the per-element branch structure. This
suggests that architectural separation of element-specific optical depth contributions
provides a useful inductive bias even without explicit masking. Second, Prism's
further improvement over Ablation Prism (1.766 vs.\ 1.932) demonstrates the additional
value of the hard masking constraint. Third, RegPayne's Jacobian penalty---a soft
regularization approach---fails to improve upon Ablation Prism's spectral fit quality
despite its explicit disentanglement objective, and its leakage ratios remain near unity
(Section~\ref{subsec:leakage}), confirming that soft penalties are insufficient to enforce
true disentanglement. Fourth, the metal-poor giant population reveals the largest
performance gap: Prism achieves a median of 1.914 versus 6.224 for The Payne and 3.225
for RegPayne, suggesting that physics constraints are most beneficial where abundance
information is concentrated in a small number of weak, specific transitions.

\section{Backward Inference on Observed Spectra}
\label{sec:backward_inference}
%------------------------------------------------------------

With Prism robustly transferring empirical patterns into its forward mapping, it naturally supports \emph{backward inference}---recovering stellar traits from newly observed spectra without training an explicit label regressor. We embed the trained Prism emulator within an optimization loop and utilize Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation to invert the spectrum-to-label process.

\subsection{Optimization Setup and Uncertainty Estimation}
\label{subsec:map_setup}
To map an observed flux $F_\mathrm{obs}$ back to underlying labels $\theta$, we minimize a joint likelihood loss combining the reconstruction fidelity (spectral pixel loss) and a parameter prior derived from a data-driven warm-start initial guess:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{MAP}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i \mathit{ivar}_i \bigl(F_{\mathrm{obs},i} - F_{\mathrm{pred},i}(\theta)\bigr)^2 + \frac{1}{2} w_\mathrm{prior} \sum_j \left(\frac{\theta_j - \mu_j}{\sigma_j}\right)^2,
\end{equation}
where $\mu_j$ and $\sigma_j$ are priors initialized by predicting labels using a lightweight warm-start Heteroscedastic Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) estimator, anchoring inferences to a physically sound neighborhood. Prism is exceptionally efficient for this gradient-based routine due to its analytic derivatives. Using XLA-accelerated Adam optimizer instances partitioned logically into Core and Element stages, we obtain a converged MAP estimate $\hat{\theta}$.

To provide robust, physically grounded confidence bounds alongside our point approximations, we bypass unstable internal curvature derivations and formally adopt the historically robust test-time uncertainties ($\sigma_\mathrm{ASPCAP}$) supplied by the established APOGEE data pipeline.

\subsection{Validation on Stratified Test Sample}
\label{subsec:map_results}
We validated the backward optimization routine extracting the 23 labels on a thoroughly stratified test sample of $N=500$ stars purposefully binned uniformly across $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log{g}$, and $[M/\mathrm{H}]$ parameters to represent a rigorous distribution.

Notably, elements like $[\mathrm{Mg/Fe}]$, $[\mathrm{Al/Fe}]$, and $[\mathrm{Mn/Fe}]$ yield highly constrained estimates showcasing RMSEs of $\sim$0.13, 0.17, and 0.24 dex respectively.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

We have presented a neural emulator for stellar spectra that combines the advantages of
data-driven modeling---training on real observations, robustness to noise and calibration
artifacts---with hard physical constraints that guarantee interpretable, disentangled label
sensitivities. The architectural separation of the spectrum into continuum, molecular, and
atomic contributions mirrors the physical origin of these spectral components and
provides a natural pathway for model interpretation.

The information-leakage test (Section~\ref{subsec:leakage}) reveals a two-stage picture of
disentanglement. The per-element branch architecture alone (Ablation Prism) recovers
approximate spectral selectivity from the training data without supervision, consistent
with the atomic physics encoded in real APOGEE spectra. This is a genuinely non-trivial
result: the training loss contains no leakage penalty, yet the model concentrates its
sensitivity near known atomic transitions. Prism's hard masks then enforce this selectivity
exactly, eliminating residual cross-element leakage. The physically meaningful finding is
therefore not Prism's guaranteed zero leakage---which follows trivially from the
architecture---but Ablation Prism's partial recovery of spectral selectivity, which
demonstrates that the data itself encodes the atomic physics our masks make explicit.

\subsubsection{Limitations}

Several limitations of the current model merit attention. First, the thermodynamic state
branch introduces a degree of flexibility that, if unconstrained, could in principle absorb
variance attributable to chemical abundances. In practice, we manage this through the
architectural constraint that the chemical expert branch receives only the state vector
(not raw labels), but more rigorous inspection via sensitivity analysis would strengthen
this claim.

Second, although our imputation procedure rigorously preserves data isolation across partitions by pre-fitting to the training set alone, it still introduces a 
correlation structure into the labels that may not completely reflect true astrophysical
covariance. This could bias the thermodynamic state vector for stars with many missing
labels. A more principled treatment, such as explicit latent variable models for missing
data, remains a natural extension.

Third, unlike The Cannon 2, which delivers formal label uncertainties via the Jacobian
of the test-step likelihood, Prism initially functioned solely as a forward emulator. However, we have since implemented a backward inference pipeline incorporating XLA-accelerated Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation to recover labels from observed spectra (see Section~\ref{sec:backward_inference}).

Fourth, being a deterministic model, Prism does not reproduce the stochastic stellar
variance that would exist among stars sharing the same parameters, such as variance arising
through non-LTE physics or magnetic activity. This limits its utility as a synthetic data
generator for population-level applications.

\subsubsection{Performance in Cool Regions}

Systematic degradation in cool regions ($T_\mathrm{eff} < 4500$\,K) is the most notable
limitation. This stems from two factors: (i) the training sample contains relatively few
extremely cool stars (${\sim}$30\% of the dataset falls below 4500\,K), so the model has
limited exposure to this regime, and (ii) molecular physics and line lists are incomplete,
meaning even perfect physical constraints cannot fully compensate for missing spectroscopic
data. This is not a deficiency of our approach per se---all baselines show similar
degradation---but rather a fundamental limitation of the underlying data.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
%------------------------------------------------------------

We outline the following planned extensions and validation experiments.

\paragraph{External validation via classifier.}
We will train a discriminative neural network to distinguish real APOGEE spectra from
Prism-generated ones. If the classifier's accuracy approaches chance level (50\%), it will
confirm that generated and real spectra are statistically indistinguishable.

\paragraph{Synthetic dataset generator.}
We will release a public Python class that allows users to specify a parameter space and
generate arbitrary numbers of synthetic spectra for downstream applications such as
training data-driven classifiers or testing spectral analysis pipelines.

\paragraph{Extension to other surveys.}
While trained on APOGEE H-band, the architecture is survey-agnostic. We envision
extensions to visible-wavelength surveys (GALAH, LAMOST) and to other infrared bands,
provided appropriate training data and spectral masks are available.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------

We have constructed a physics-constrained generative neural emulator for APOGEE DR17
stellar spectra. We train Prism on 149,966 real observations and achieve
a median reduced $\chi^2_\mathrm{red} = 1.766$ on a held-out test set of $\sim$9,966
stars, outperforming all four baselines (Table~\ref{tab:chi2}). By decomposing the
predicted optical depth into physically motivated branches and enforcing element-specific
weighted spectral masks, we guarantee that each chemical abundance label affects only those
pixels associated with its known atomic transitions.

An information-leakage analysis reveals a two-stage picture: Ablation Prism's per-element
branch architecture already recovers approximate spectral selectivity from the training
data without supervision, while Prism's hard masks enforce this selectivity exactly.
The physically meaningful result is that real APOGEE spectra encode sufficient element-specific
information for an unconstrained model to recover approximate disentanglement---and that
hard architectural constraints then eliminate the residual leakage, with measurable benefit
to spectral fit quality.

Prism outperforms The Payne, Expanded Payne, Ablation Prism, and RegPayne in reduced
$\chi^2$ while providing provable disentanglement that unconstrained baselines cannot
achieve. The improvement is largest in the metal-poor giant regime (median $\chi^2_\mathrm{red}$
of 1.914 versus 6.224 for The Payne), where abundance information is concentrated in weak,
element-specific transitions that benefit most from architectural enforcement of physical
selectivity. Prism represents a step toward spectral emulators that are simultaneously
accurate, physically transparent, and faithful to the observational properties of the
instruments that collect the data.

All code and trained model weights are available in our project repository, with
comprehensive documentation and usage examples. We welcome community contributions and
discussion via GitHub.
